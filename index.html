New York City · civil-service hiring

Passed the exam. Then the machine.

New York City fills most of its permanent jobs through a competitive civil-service system: an exam, a ranked list, a certification, an appointment. Three public datasets track the stock, the flow and the outcome — and together they show where the pipeline clogs.

01  The stock

Right now, hundreds of thousands of people are waiting on a list

A civil-service list is everyone who passed a given exam, ranked by score. A list stays active one to four years. These are the people currently eligible to be hired.

02  The pipeline

Three stages, and a wide gap between passing and hired

An eligible list is the raw material. When an agency has a vacancy it requests a certification — a slice of the ranked list sent over for consideration. Only some of those people are ever appointed. The rest leave the list for reasons the city itself records.

03  Over-certification

09  Sources & method

How this was built

The three datasets

  • Stock Civil Service List (Active) — every candidate on a live eligible list.
  • Flow Civil Service List Certification — every certification sent to an agency.
  • Outcome Eligible List Utilization (Local Law 50 of 2004) — appointments and every reason candidates left lists.

Key calculations

  • Certified per vacancy. The certification file repeats its header counts (vacancies, requested, certified) on every candidate row. Summing those raw would massively overcount, so each certification is collapsed to one record before any ratio. The citywide figure is total certified ÷ total vacancies; the "typical" figure is the median of the per-certification ratio.
  • Provisional share. Share of certifications flagged as a provisional replacement in the source field.
  • List life & request lag. List life is established-to-expiration (or extension) per list; the request lag is request-date to certification-date, medianed across certifications.
  • Fall-off. Summed removal-reason counts from the utilization file; "appointed off another list" is excluded from fall-off because it is still a hire.

Limitations & confidence

  • These files carry no demographic data — no race, sex or age — so this dashboard makes no equity claims. Candidate names are present in the source but are aggregated here and never displayed.
  • A tall certified-per-vacancy number does not mean many candidates were genuinely in contention — the "rule of three" and candidate willingness narrow the real field. Treat over-certification as workload, not competition.
  • The utilization file counts candidate-removals cumulatively across years and is keyed by title-year, not by individual; totals are directional, not a person-level census.
  • A certification can span more than one title; title and agency labels use the dominant value on each certification.
  • Numbers refresh when DCAS updates the source datasets (roughly weekly). Figures are current as of the build date shown at the top.
