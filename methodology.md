# The civil-service hiring pipeline — methodology

An analysis of how New York City hires through its competitive civil-service system, built from three of the city's own datasets: the stock of eligible candidates, the flow of certifications to agencies and the outcomes (who was appointed, and why others left lists).

Everything on the dashboard is computed directly from the sources below by `build.py` (Python standard library only, no API token). Run `python3 build.py` to rebuild the baked JSON in `data/`.

## Sources

All from NYC Open Data, published by the Department of Citywide Administrative Services (DCAS).

| Role | Dataset | ID |
|---|---|---|
| Stock | [Civil Service List (Active)](https://data.cityofnewyork.us/dataset/vx8i-nprf) | `vx8i-nprf` |
| Flow | [Civil Service List Certification](https://data.cityofnewyork.us/dataset/a9md-ynri) | `a9md-ynri` |
| Outcome | [Eligible List Utilization (Local Law 50 of 2004)](https://data.cityofnewyork.us/dataset/qjzt-ytn9) | `qjzt-ytn9` |

## How the system works

1. **Exam.** A candidate takes a competitive civil-service exam.
2. **List.** Everyone who passes is placed on a ranked eligible list. A list stays active for no less than one and no more than four years.
3. **Certification.** When an agency has a budgeted vacancy, it requests a *certification* — DCAS sends a slice of the ranked list for consideration.
4. **Appointment.** Under the "rule of three," an agency may appoint any of the top three willing and reachable candidates for each opening. Others remain on the list or are removed.

The three datasets map onto stages 2, 3 and 4.

## Key calculations

- **Certified per vacancy.** The certification dataset stores certification-level counts (`no_vacancies`, `no_requested`, `no_certified`) but repeats them on *every candidate row* of the certification. Summing the raw rows overcounts by orders of magnitude (a naive sum of `no_vacancies` returns ~560 million). Each certification is therefore collapsed to one record (`GROUP BY cert_issue_no`, taking the max of each header) before any ratio is computed.
  - *Citywide ratio* = total certified ÷ total vacancies across all certifications.
  - *Typical (median) ratio* = the median of the per-certification certified-÷-vacancy ratio. This is higher than the citywide figure because a handful of very large class hires (with many vacancies) pull the weighted average down.
- **Provisional share.** Share of certifications whose `provisional_replacement` field indicates the certification replaces a provisional (non-permanent) employee.
- **List life.** For each list (`exam_no`), established date to expiration (`anniversary_date`, or `extension_date` when extended); reported as the median across lists.
- **Request lag.** `request_date` to `cert_date` per certification, reported as the median. This turns out to be very short (a few days) — issuing certifications is not the bottleneck.
- **Fall-off.** Removal-reason counts from the utilization file, summed across all title-year rows. "Appointed off another list" (`aol_cnt`) is excluded from fall-off totals because it is still a hire.

## Limitations and confidence

- **No demographics.** None of these files contain race, sex or age. This dashboard makes **no equity claims**. Candidate names appear in the source data but are aggregated here and never displayed.
- **Over-certification is workload, not competition.** A large certified-per-vacancy number reflects how many names an agency must process, not how many people genuinely competed — the rule of three and candidate willingness sharply narrow the real field. Treat the ratio as administrative burden.
- **Utilization counts are directional.** The Local Law 50 file is keyed by title and reporting year and counts removals cumulatively; it is not a person-level census. Totals show scale and proportion, not exact individuals.
- **Certifications can span titles.** A single certification may cover more than one list; title and agency labels use the dominant value on each certification.
- **Freshness.** DCAS refreshes the source datasets on roughly a weekly cadence. All figures are current as of the build date shown on the dashboard.
